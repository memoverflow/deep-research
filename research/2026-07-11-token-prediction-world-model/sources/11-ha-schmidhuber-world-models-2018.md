## Key Content
- The foundational "World Models" paper that established the modern ML usage of the term, predating the LLM-era debate.
- Trains a generative recurrent neural network (a VAE + RNN/MDN-RNN combo) UNSUPERVISED to model the transitions of popular RL environments (e.g. CarRacing, VizDoom), learning a compressed spatio-temporal latent representation of the environment purely from raw pixel observations and actions.
- Key idea directly relevant to the token-prediction debate: a compact, learned latent-variable model that captures "what tends to happen next" in an environment, learned purely from predicting future observations (unsupervised sequence modeling), counts as a legitimate "world model" — even though it was never given explicit physics/rules, exactly analogous to how Othello-GPT/Chess-GPT are never given explicit game rules.
- Demonstrates that once you have this compressed world model, you can train very small, simple policies (controllers) on top of it via evolution strategies, and even train the policy entirely "inside its own hallucinated dream" generated by the world model (train in simulation-of-a-simulation) — showing the learned latent state is causally useful for downstream control, not just descriptively accurate.
- Historical significance: this paper's title is literally why the term "world model" is used the way it is in later LLM interpretability work — it establishes the RL/predictive-coding lineage (Ha & Schmidhuber) that Kenneth Li's Othello-GPT paper and the survey (source 10) explicitly build on and distinguish from LeCun's later, richer, grounded notion.
